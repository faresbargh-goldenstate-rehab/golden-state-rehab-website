I Was Terrified to Ask for Help. Here's What I Wish I'd Known.

A Golden State Rehab alum shares the story of the phone call that changed everything — and the five fears that almost stopped them from making it.

A Golden State Rehab Alum
Recovery · As told to our team
• May 14, 2026 5 min read Medically reviewed by Dr. Eric Chaghouri, MD

I need to tell you about the longest phone call I never made — the dozens of times I picked up my phone, stared at the number for a treatment center, and put it back down. If you're doing that right now, reading this instead of calling, I get it. I was you for almost two years. So let me just tell you what I wish someone had told me.

Fear #1: "If I call, that makes it real."

This was the big one. As long as I didn't say it out loud to a professional, I could keep telling myself it wasn't that bad. Here's the thing nobody warned me about: it was already real. The only thing my silence protected was the addiction. Saying it out loud didn't make it worse — it was the first time in years it started to get better.

Fear #2: "They're going to judge me."

I had this whole speech prepared to defend myself. I didn't need a word of it. The person who answered had heard my story a hundred times — not because my story is common in a bad way, but because shame is. Nobody flinched. Nobody lectured. They just asked how they could help.

I spent two years afraid of a conversation that turned out to be the kindest one I'd had in a decade.

Fear #3: "I'll have to drop my entire life."

I assumed treatment meant disappearing for a month. It didn't. I did an intensive outpatient program — I still slept in my own bed, kept working, stayed in my kids' lives. The schedule was built around my life, not the other way around. That alone removed the excuse I'd been hiding behind for two years.

Fear #4: "What if it doesn't work for someone like me?"

I was convinced I was a special case — too far gone, too complicated, too whatever. Turns out the "complicated" part was that I also had untreated anxiety I'd been drinking to quiet. When they treated both at once, the whole thing finally made sense. I wasn't a lost cause. I was a person who'd never been treated for the right things.

Fear #5: "Asking for help means I'm weak."

I'll be blunt: making that call was the single hardest and bravest thing I have ever done. Everything I'm proud of now is downstream of it. If "weak" is picking up the phone when every cell in your body is screaming not to, then I'll take weak. It saved my life.

If you're still holding the phone You don't have to have the words ready. You don't have to be sure. You just have to dial. The person on the other end will take it from there — that's their whole job.

I don't know your story. But I know what it feels like to stand at the edge of that call. If you're there right now, do the thing I waited two years to do. Make it real. Let someone help you carry it.
